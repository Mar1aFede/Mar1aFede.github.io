Research

My current research lies at the intersection of the philosophy of science, philosophy of AI, and data- and computational philosophy. I am especially interested in the epistemology of machine learningâ€”how models learn from data and under what conditions such learning yields scientific knowledge.

In my dissertation, I examine whether and how machine learning can contribute to scientific knowledge. The project brings together topics such as: automated scientific discovery and the prospect of closed-loop inquiry; the distinction between data and phenomena (e.g., Bogen & Woodward) and its bearing on ML practice; the role of synthetic versus observational data; the relationship between models and theories (including concerns about explanation, understanding, and causation, e.g., Pearl); and the limits of purely data-driven discovery. I argue for a nuanced empiricism that recognizes the dynamic interplay between data, models, and domains, and I explore how recent deep learning practice reshapes traditional debates about discovery, prediction vs. accommodation, and the logic of scientific inquiry.

On the technical side, part of my work studies generalization and simplicity in deep learning via pruning. Building on the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis, I introduce a Noiseâ€‘Cancelling Razor to capture dynamic simplicity: subnetworks that remain stable under controlled perturbations (e.g., injected noise at validation) tend to generalize better. This links philosophical accounts of simplicity to concrete training dynamics in neural networks.
